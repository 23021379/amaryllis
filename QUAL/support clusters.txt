### **1. The "Electoral Register" Trap**

You asked: *"Does the electoral register tell me the number of people aged 18-24 in a household or if they are christian?"*

**The Answer is NO.**

The Open Register contains exactly two things:
1.  **Name.**
2.  **Address.**

It does **not** contain:
*   Age (except for "Attainers" turning 18 that year).
*   Religion.
*   Voting History.
*   Household Composition.

If you buy the raw register, you will get a list of names. You will have no idea if they are an 18-year-old Green Party voter or an 80-year-old Conservative.

**The "Enhanced" Register:**
Companies like **Experian (Mosaic)** and **CACI (Acorn)** buy the Open Register and *enrich* it with credit card data, loyalty card usage, and modelled assumptions.
*   *Cost:* Extremely high (£10k-£50k+ depending on volume).
*   *Utility:* They can give you "Likely Demographic" flags, but for a custom ML project, this is black-box data you cannot validate.

### **2. Your Current Dataset: The "Granularity" Problem**

The CSV snippet you provided contains **Constituency Level Data** (`E14...` = Westminster Parliamentary Constituency).

**Why this is dangerous:**
*   **The Scope:** A Constituency like "Aldershot" contains ~75,000 voters.
*   **The Fallacy:** You are seeing that Aldershot has "16% over 65s."
*   **The Reality:** The town centre might be 5% retirees, while the rural village (where your solar farm is) might be 40% retirees.
*   **The Risk:** If you extrapolate the Constituency average to the village, your model will underestimate the "NIMBY Coefficient" by a factor of 3.

**You need to go deeper.**

### **3. The Solution: Output Areas (The "Street" Level)**

You do not need to buy data. The ONS provides Census 2021 data at the **Output Area (OA)** level.

*   **What is an OA?** The smallest unit of census data. It covers about **125 households** (roughly one or two streets).
*   **Granularity:** It is fine-grained enough to differentiate a "Retirement Village" from a "Student Halls" next door.

**The Strategy: "Propensity Mapping"**
Instead of knowing exactly who lives at *12 High Street* (illegal/impossible), you characterize the *street*.

**Action:** Go to **Nomis** and download the following tables at **Output Area (OA)** level:

#### **A. The "Youth" Vector (Targeting 18-24s)**
*   **Table:** `TS007A - Age structure`.
*   **Target:** Sum of Age 18, 19, 20, 21-24 columns.
*   **Application:** If an OA has >30% in this bracket, these are likely renters/students. They rarely object to planning. *Do not waste budget mailing them unless you are mobilizing support.*

#### **B. The "Household Composition" Vector**
*   **Table:** `TS003 - Household composition`.
*   **Target:** `One person household: Aged 66 years and over`.
*   **The "Lonely NIMBY" Risk:** High concentrations of solitary elderly homeowners are statistically the highest volume writers of objection letters.
*   **Strategy:** Send these streets a "Reassurance Leaflet" focusing on *visual screening* and *no construction noise*.

#### **C. The "Values" Vector (Religion)**
*   **Table:** `TS030 - Religion`.
*   **Target:** `Christian` vs `No Religion`.
*   **Correlation:** "No Religion" correlates strongly with younger, progressive demographics who are pro-renewables. "Christian" (in the Census context) often correlates with traditionalism and rural conservationism.
